Electric charge is quantised: charge comes in integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C
- Conservation of charge: total charge in an isolated system is constant
- Two types: positive and negative; like charges repel, unlike charges attract
- Charge transfer by friction, electrostatic induction, and contact; grounding allows charge to flow to/from Earth

**Millikan's oil drop experiment** provided evidence for charge quantisation:
- Oil drop suspended between parallel plates when electric force balances gravity: $qE = mg$
- With $E = \frac{V}{d}$, find $q = \frac{mgd}{V}$
- Repeating for many drops showed charge is always an integer multiple of $e$
- Ignoring buoyancy overestimates the electric force, hence overestimates $q$

**Coulomb's law**: $$F = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2}, \quad k = \frac{1}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}$$
- Point charges or spherical charge distributions (treat as point at centre)
- $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C$^2$ N$^{-1}$ m$^{-2}$ is permittivity of free space
- For other media, replace $\varepsilon_0$ with $\varepsilon = \varepsilon_r \varepsilon_0$

Electric field strength is force per unit positive test charge: $$E = \frac{F}{q}$$
- For a point charge: $E = k\frac{Q}{r^2}$
- Between parallel plates (uniform field): $E = \frac{V}{d}$
- Direction: the direction a small positive test charge would be pushed

Electric field lines:
- Point away from positive charges, toward negative charges
- Density of lines represents field strength
- Lines never cross
- Radial for point charges, uniform and parallel between parallel plates
- Edge effects: field lines curve outward near edges of parallel plates, so field is non-uniform there; only truly uniform in the central region
- Required patterns: single point charge, two point charges (same/opposite), parallel plates, inside/outside conducting sphere

Magnetic fields:
- Created by permanent magnets and by moving charges (currents)
- Field lines form closed loops: from north to south outside the magnet, south to north inside
- Lines never cross; density represents field strength
- Right-hand rule for straight wire: thumb = current direction, curled fingers = field direction
- Right-hand rule for solenoid/coil: curled fingers = current direction, thumb = north pole / field inside
- Required patterns: bar magnet, straight wire (concentric circles), circular coil, solenoid (uniform inside, bar-magnet-like outside)

**HL Additional Content**

Electric potential energy is the work done to assemble a system of charges from infinite separation: $$E_p = k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r}$$
- Include signs of charges: positive $E_p$ for like charges (work done to push them together), negative for unlike (work released)
- Belongs to the system of charges, not individual charges
- Defined as zero at infinite separation

Electric potential $V_e$ at a point is the work done per unit charge to bring a small positive test charge from infinity to that point: $$V_e = k\frac{Q}{r}$$
- Scalar quantity (can be positive or negative depending on sign of source charge $Q$)
- Defined as zero at infinity
- Positive charge → positive potential (positive work to bring $+q$ closer)
- Negative charge → negative potential (positive work to separate $+q$ from it)

Equipotential surfaces:
- All points at the same potential; perpendicular to field lines everywhere
- No work done moving a charge along an equipotential surface ($\Delta V_e = 0$)
- For a point charge: concentric spheres; spacing increases further from charge (weaker field)
- For parallel plates: equally spaced planes parallel to the plates
- Required: point charge, up to four point charges, inside/outside solid and hollow conducting spheres, parallel plates

Work done moving charge $q$ through potential difference $\Delta V_e$: $$W = q\Delta V_e$$
- Electronvolt: $1 \text{ eV} = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ J (work done moving one electron charge through 1 V)

Electric potential gradient gives field strength: $$E = -\frac{\Delta V_e}{\Delta r}$$
- Field points in the direction of decreasing potential
- Steeper gradient = stronger field
- On a $V_e$ vs $r$ graph, the magnitude of the gradient at any point gives $E$

Charged conducting sphere (radius $R$):
- Outside ($r > R$): behaves like a point charge, $E = k\frac{Q}{r^2}$, $V_e = k\frac{Q}{r}$
- At surface ($r = R$): maximum $E$, $V_e = k\frac{Q}{R}$
- Inside ($r < R$): $E = 0$, $V_e$ is constant and equal to $V_e$ at the surface
- Same applies for a hollow conducting sphere
